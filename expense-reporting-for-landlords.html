Common Rental Property Expenses

Small landlords (i.e., you own property under your name and have no employees) need two types of records: a record of rental income and expenses and supporting documents to prove your income and expenses in case of an IRS audit. One of the biggest dos and one of the biggest don'ts of expense reporting is to list income and expenses for each property. Don't mix them. Below are many of the landlord expenses you need to track.

  • City or county business permit expenses
  • Closing costs of your real estate financing
  • Dwelling fire (DP3) insurance policy
  • Homeowner association, broker, legal, accounting and bookkeeping fees
  • Interest payments
  • Lodging tax for short-term rentals
  • Maintenance costs -- roofs, pest control, lawn care, etc.
  • Marketing expenses
  • Property and income taxes
  • Property upgrades and builder's risk insurance
  • Tenant screening
  • Unclaimed depreciation expense
  • Unplanned repairs and vacancies

Landlord Tips and Tricks for Expense Reporting

Like a lot of aspects of rental property management, accounting gets more complicated the more properties you hold. Between rent and all of the expenses listed above, you'll need a detailed ledger. However, the right tools and a few tricks can simplify the process to save you time and sanity at the end of the year.

  • Open a separate bank account for each property you own.
  • Set up automatic payments for recurring expenses.
  • Take pictures of your receipts or use a scanning app.
  • Invest in secure cloud storage so you can access all of your documents wherever you are.
  • Get specialized landlord accounting software to help with the heavy lifting.
  • If you plan to write off mileage at tax time, download a mileage tracker app to record all of your business drives.
